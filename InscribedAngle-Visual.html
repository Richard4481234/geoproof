The Inscribed Angle Theorem

Take a circle with center O, and a fixed arc between two points A and B. The central angle ∠AOB opens onto that arc from the center. The inscribed angle ∠APB opens onto the same arc from a point P elsewhere on the circle. The theorem says:

∠AOB = 2 × ∠APB

The central angle is always exactly twice any inscribed angle that subtends the same arc.

Angles in the same arc are equal

Because every inscribed angle on the same arc equals half of the one fixed central angle, they all equal each other — no matter where P sits on its arc. Drag P around and watch its angle stay put while you move it.

Special case: Thales' theorem

When A and B are diametrically opposite, the arc is a semicircle and the central angle is a straight 180°. The inscribed angle is then exactly 180° ÷ 2 = 90°. So any triangle drawn on a diameter, with its third point on the circle, is right-angled. Drag A and B until they line up through O to see it.

When P crosses to the other arc

Move P across A or B to the opposite arc and it now looks at the other arc, whose central angle is the reflex one (more than 180°). The doubling still holds — the inscribed angle simply becomes the supplement of the first, which is exactly why opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral add to 180°.

Why it's true (sketch)

Draw the radius from O to P. It splits the figure into two isosceles triangles (two sides are radii). The exterior-angle rule applied to each one shows the angle at O is twice the angle at P. Adding the two pieces gives the full result.

A bit of history

This appears as Proposition 20 in Book III of Euclid's Elements (c. 300 BCE). Its semicircle corollary is named for Thales of Miletus (c. 624–546 BCE), traditionally the first result said to be formally proved.
