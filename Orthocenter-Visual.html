The Orthocenter

An altitude of a triangle is the segment from a vertex straight to the opposite side, meeting it at a right angle. Every triangle has three altitudes, and they always pass through a single point — the orthocenter H:

the three altitudes meet at the orthocenter H

It is one of the four classic triangle centers, alongside the centroid, incenter, and circumcenter.

Inside, on, or outside

For an acute triangle the orthocenter is inside. For a right triangle it lands exactly on the right-angle vertex (two of the altitudes are the legs themselves). For an obtuse triangle it lies outside the triangle, and the feet of two altitudes fall on extensions of the sides — turn on "Side extensions" to see them.

A surprising symmetry

Any one of the four points A, B, C, H is the orthocenter of the triangle formed by the other three. The four points form an "orthocentric system."

On the Euler line

The orthocenter lies on the Euler line together with the centroid G and circumcenter O, with HG = 2·GO. The midpoint of OH is the center of the nine-point circle.

A note on the name

"Ortho-" means right or perpendicular — fitting, since the orthocenter is built entirely from perpendiculars to the sides.
