The Law of Sines

In any triangle, the ratio of a side to the sine of its opposite angle is the same for all three sides:

a / sin A = b / sin B = c / sin C

That shared ratio always equals 2R — the diameter of the triangle's circumscribed circle (the unique circle passing through all three vertices).

Why a circle shows up

This follows from the inscribed angle theorem: for any chord of a circle, the angle it subtends from any point on the circle is fixed, and a chord's length relates to that angle and the circle's radius in exactly this way. Toggle "Circumcircle" above to see it drawn live.

Special case: right triangles

When an angle is 90°, sin of that angle is 1, so the side opposite it equals 2R exactly — the hypotenuse is the circle's diameter, and the circumcenter sits exactly at its midpoint. Drag a vertex until the badge reads "Right triangle" and watch the center dot land on the midpoint of the longest side.

When to use it

The Law of Sines solves triangles when you know two angles and a side (AAS/ASA), or two sides and a non-included angle (SSA, the "ambiguous case" — sometimes two different triangles fit). Contrast with the Law of Cosines, which handles SAS and SSS instead.

A classic example

If A = 40° and a = 10, then 2R = 10 / sin 40° ≈ 15.56. If B = 75°, then b = 2R · sin 75° ≈ 15.03.

A bit of history

Ancient astronomers like Ptolemy worked with closely related chord relationships centuries earlier, but a clear general statement of the law of sines for plane triangles is often credited to Persian polymath Nasir al-Din al-Tusi in the 13th century, with further development by later European mathematicians such as Regiomontanus.
