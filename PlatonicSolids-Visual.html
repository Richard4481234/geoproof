The Platonic Solids

A Platonic solid is a convex polyhedron whose faces are all the same regular polygon, with the same number of faces meeting at every vertex. That strict regularity is extremely demanding — and remarkably, there are exactly five:

tetrahedron · cube · octahedron · dodecahedron · icosahedron

The Schläfli symbol {p, q}

Each solid is captured by two numbers: {p, q} means the faces are regular p-gons, with q of them meeting at each vertex. A cube is {4, 3} — squares, three to a corner.

Why only five

At a corner you need at least three faces, and their angles must total less than 360° (or the corner flattens). Regular triangles (60°) allow 3, 4 or 5 around a vertex; squares (90°) and pentagons (108°) allow only 3; six triangles, four squares, or three hexagons already reach 360°. That leaves exactly five possibilities.

Duals

Put a vertex at the center of each face and connect neighbours: you get another Platonic solid, the dual. The cube and octahedron are duals, as are the dodecahedron and icosahedron; the tetrahedron is its own dual. Dual partners swap the roles of faces and vertices — notice their V and F counts trade places.

A bit of history

Known to the ancient Greeks and central to Plato's Timaeus (c. 360 BCE), where he tied them to the classical elements. Euclid's Elements closes, in Book XIII, by constructing all five and proving there are no others.
