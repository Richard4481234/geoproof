A right triangle ABC has its right angle at C. The vertex C stays on the circle whose diameter is the hypotenuse AB, which keeps the angle at C exactly 90 degrees. An altitude is dropped from C straight down to the hypotenuse, meeting it at foot D and splitting the hypotenuse into two parts: p from A to D and q from D to B. Three geometric-mean relations hold: the altitude squared equals p times q; leg AC squared equals p times the whole hypotenuse; leg BC squared equals q times the whole hypotenuse. You can drag A and B along the base and drag C around the circle. All three relations are shown live and stay true. An info button opens a drawer with the explanation.

Geometric Mean Theorem

Drop the altitude from the right angle of a right triangle to the hypotenuse. It splits the hypotenuse into p and q and creates three similar triangles, giving three "geometric mean" relations:

h² = p·q  •  b² = p·c  •  a² = q·c

Here the altitude is h, its foot splits hypotenuse c into p (next to A) and q (next to B), and a = BC, b = AC are the legs.

"Geometric mean"

h = √(p·q): the altitude is the geometric mean of the two hypotenuse pieces. Each leg is the geometric mean of the whole hypotenuse and the piece touching it.

Why the right angle is automatic

C rides on the circle whose diameter is AB. By Thales' theorem, any point on that circle sees the diameter at a right angle — so ∠C is always 90°.

A bonus

Add the two leg relations: b² + a² = pc + qc = (p+q)c = c². That's the Pythagorean theorem, falling straight out of these.
