Euler's Polyhedron Formula

Count the corners (vertices V), the edges E and the flat faces F of any convex polyhedron. Whichever solid you pick, the same combination always comes out to two:

V − E + F = 2

A cube: 8 − 12 + 6 = 2. A dodecahedron: 20 − 30 + 12 = 2. It never fails.

Why it's remarkable

The three counts vary wildly from solid to solid, yet this particular mix of them is a fixed invariant. It holds not just for the regular solids but for every convex polyhedron — prisms, pyramids, soccer balls, anything you can inflate into a sphere.

The number 2

That 2 is the “Euler characteristic” of the sphere. Change the topology and it changes: a polyhedron shaped like a doughnut (one hole) gives V − E + F = 0. The formula is a first glimpse of topology — geometry that survives bending and stretching.

A quick proof idea

Remove one face and flatten the rest onto the plane into a network of regions. Adding the edges and vertices back one at a time, each step keeps V − E + F unchanged; the flattened network gives V − E + F = 1, and restoring the removed face makes 2.

A bit of history

Leonhard Euler stated it in 1750–1752. Descartes had found a closely related result a century earlier, so it is sometimes called the Descartes–Euler formula.
