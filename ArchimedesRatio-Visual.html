Cone : Sphere : Cylinder = 1 : 2 : 3

Take a cone, a sphere and a cylinder that all share the same radius r, where the cone and cylinder have height equal to the diameter, 2r. Their volumes fall into a strikingly simple pattern:

Vcone : Vsphere : Vcylinder = 1 : 2 : 3

Spelled out: the cone is ⅓·π·r²·(2r) = ⅔·π·r³, the sphere is ⁴⁄₃·π·r³, and the cylinder is π·r²·(2r) = 2·π·r³. Dividing through by ⅔·π·r³ gives 1 : 2 : 3.

A beautiful consequence

Since the cone is one third of the cylinder and the sphere is two thirds, the cone and the sphere together exactly fill the cylinder: 1 + 2 = 3.

How Archimedes found the sphere

This is how the sphere's volume was first discovered. Archimedes showed the sphere is two-thirds of its circumscribing cylinder, which gives V = ⁴⁄₃·π·r³. He considered it his finest result — and asked for a sphere-in-a-cylinder to be carved on his tombstone. Turn on “Nest sphere in cylinder” to see it.

Why the ratio never changes

Every term is a constant times r³, so scaling r rescales all three volumes together — the 1 : 2 : 3 ratio is the same for a marble or a planet.

A bit of history

Archimedes of Syracuse (c. 287–212 BCE) proved it in On the Sphere and Cylinder, long before calculus, using his “method of exhaustion.”
