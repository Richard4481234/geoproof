A five-pointed star (pentagram) is drawn from five points, each of which you can drag. The star is made by connecting every second point. At each of the five tips there is a point angle. No matter how the five points are arranged, as long as they form a proper star, the five tip angles always add up to 180 degrees. Each tip angle is marked and its value shown, along with their running total, which stays at 180. An info button opens a drawer with the explanation.

∠1 + ∠2 + ∠3 + ∠4 + ∠5 = 180°

The Star's Angle Sum

Take any five-pointed star drawn in one stroke (a pentagram). Add up the angles at its five points:

∠1 + ∠2 + ∠3 + ∠4 + ∠5 = 180°

It doesn't matter whether the star is regular or lopsided — the total is always 180°, the same as a single triangle.

Why it holds

Each tip is the apex of a small triangle whose base is a chord of the star. Chasing the base angles around the inner pentagon, every interior angle cancels in pairs, and what survives is exactly one triangle's worth of angle — 180°.

The bigger pattern

For a star polygon written {n/k} (connect every k-th of n points), the tip angles sum to 180°·(n − 2k). The five-pointed star is {5/2}, giving 180·(5 − 4) = 180°.

Keep it a star

The result needs a proper star: keep the five points in convex position (none tucked inside the others). The badge warns you if the shape stops being a clean pentagram.
