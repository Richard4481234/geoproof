A Rhombus's Diagonals

A rhombus is a quadrilateral with four equal sides. Its diagonals do something special:

AC ⊥ BD, and they bisect each other

The diagonals cross at right angles, and each cuts the other exactly in half.

Why perpendicular

Write the two sides at A as vectors u and v with |u| = |v| (equal sides). The diagonals are u + v and v − u. Their dot product is (u+v)·(v−u) = |v|² − |u|² = 0 — so they're perpendicular.

Bonus: angle bisectors

Each diagonal also bisects the two vertex angles it runs between, which is why the diagonals split the rhombus into four congruent right triangles.

Where it sits

A rhombus is a parallelogram (so the diagonals already bisect each other) plus equal sides (which adds the perpendicularity). A square is a rhombus whose diagonals are also equal.
