The Angle Bisector (as a locus)

The angle bisector is the ray that cuts an angle into two equal halves. Its key property is about distance to the two arms (sides) of the angle:

a point is equidistant from both arms Ō¤║ it lies on the angle bisector

"Distance to an arm" means the perpendicular distance ŌĆö the shortest hop from the point straight to that line.

Both directions

Slide P along the bisector and the two perpendicular distances stay equal. And any point that is the same distance from both arms has to lie on the bisector ŌĆö there is nowhere else.

Why it's true

Drop perpendiculars from P to the two arms. With the shared segment VP and the equal split angles at V, the two right triangles are congruent, so the two perpendicular distances match. The converse runs the same argument backwards.

The companion result

This mirrors the perpendicular bisector, which is the set of points equidistant from two points. Here it's two lines. The three angle bisectors of a triangle meet at the incenter ŌĆö the point equidistant from all three sides, and the center of the inscribed circle.
