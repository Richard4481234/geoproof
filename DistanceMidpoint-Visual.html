A coordinate grid with x and y axes is shown. Two points A and B sit on the grid and snap to whole-number coordinates; drag them. The segment AB is drawn, along with a right triangle whose horizontal leg is the change in x and whose vertical leg is the change in y. The distance from A to B is the square root of the sum of those squared changes. The midpoint of AB is the average of the two points' coordinates. Both the distance and the midpoint are shown live with the numbers from the current points. An info button opens a drawer with the formulas.

Distance & Midpoint

On the coordinate plane, two points A = (x₁, y₁) and B = (x₂, y₂) have a distance and a midpoint given by simple formulas:

distance = √((x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²)
midpoint = ( (x₁+x₂)/2 , (y₁+y₂)/2 )

Distance is the Pythagorean theorem

The horizontal gap Δx = x₂−x₁ and the vertical gap Δy = y₂−y₁ are the two legs of a right triangle whose hypotenuse is the segment AB. So AB² = Δx² + Δy² — the distance formula is just the Pythagorean theorem in coordinates. Turn on "Right triangle" to see the legs.

Midpoint is the average

The midpoint sits halfway along AB, so each of its coordinates is simply the average of the corresponding coordinates of A and B.

Try it

Drag A and B (they snap to whole-number grid points). A 3-4-5 example: put A at (0,0) and B at (3,4) — the distance reads 5, and the midpoint is (1.5, 2).
