Vertical Angles

When two straight lines cross, they form four angles. The pairs of angles directly opposite each other — the vertical (or vertically opposite) angles — are always equal:

opposite angles are equal · adjacent angles add to 180°

The two angles of one colour here are a vertical pair; the two of the other colour are the second vertical pair.

Why opposite angles are equal

Each pair of neighbouring angles sits on a straight line, so each pair adds to 180°. Two angles that are both supplementary to the same third angle must be equal to each other — and that's exactly the two opposite angles.

Adjacent angles

Any two angles that share an arm (next to each other) form a straight line together, so they are supplementary — they sum to 180°. Switch to "Adjacent (linear) pairs" to see this.

A special case

If the lines are perpendicular, all four angles equal 90°. Rotate a line until the readout shows 90° and the two colours meet at the same value.

Where it's used

Vertical angles are one of the first tools for angle-chasing in geometry proofs, and they underpin the parallel-line angle relationships and many constructions.
