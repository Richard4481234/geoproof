The Triangle Inequality

For any triangle with sides a, b, and c, each side must be shorter than the sum of the other two:

a + b > c  ·  b + c > a  ·  a + c > b

If even one of those three checks fails, the lengths simply cannot close up into a triangle — there's no way to connect them.

Why it has to be true

The shortest path between two points is a straight line. So a route from A to B that detours through C (length b + a) can never be shorter than going straight from A to B (length c) — it can only be longer, or equal if C happens to sit exactly on the segment.

The construction in this tool

Side c is drawn as a fixed base between two anchor points. The third vertex must be exactly distance b from one end and exactly distance a from the other — so it has to lie on both a circle of radius b and a circle of radius a. Where those two dashed circles cross is the only place a real triangle can form. This is the classic compass-and-straightedge way to build a triangle from three given side lengths (an "SSS" construction).

The degenerate case

When a + b equals c exactly (or |a − b| equals c exactly), the two circles touch at just one point — right on the line through the two anchors. The "triangle" flattens to a straight segment with zero area. Try dragging the third vertex toward the base, or set the inputs so one sum exactly matches the third side.

Why dragging alone can't break it

Any three actual points in the plane automatically obey the inequality — that's just what distance means. So dragging the third vertex can approach equality (the degenerate, flattened case) but never cross it. The only way to see an impossible combination here is to type in three lengths that don't fit, which is exactly what the circles-not-meeting view shows.

Quick check

13, 14, 15 (the default shown) works: 13 + 14 = 27 > 15. But 5, 5, 10 lands exactly on the boundary (5 + 5 = 10 — degenerate), and 5, 5, 11 is impossible (5 + 5 = 10 < 11).

A bit of history

Euclid's Elements (Book I, Proposition 20) proves this around 300 BCE: "In any triangle, two sides taken together in any manner are greater than the remaining one."
