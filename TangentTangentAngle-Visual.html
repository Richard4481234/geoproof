The Tangent–Tangent Angle

From a point P outside a circle, exactly two tangent lines can be drawn, touching the circle at A and B. The angle between them relates to the arcs they cut off:

∠APB = ½ (far arc − near arc)

Equivalently, the tangent angle and the central angle ∠AOB of the near arc are supplementary:

∠APB + ∠AOB = 180°

Why supplementary

O, A, P, B form a kite. Each radius meets its tangent at 90° (OA ⟂ PA and OB ⟂ PB), so the four angles of quadrilateral OAPB are ∠AOB, 90°, ∠APB, 90°. They must total 360°, which leaves ∠APB + ∠AOB = 180°.

Equal tangents

The two tangent segments are always equal: PA = PB = √(OP² − R²). The line OP is an axis of symmetry — it bisects both the tangent angle at P and the central angle at O.

Moving P

Push P far away and the tangents become nearly parallel, so ∠APB shrinks toward 0° while the near arc grows toward a semicircle. Bring P close to the circle and ∠APB opens toward 180°. At every position the two angles still sum to 180°.

Half the difference of arcs

This is the "external angle" rule for circles: an angle formed outside a circle by two tangents (or secants, or one of each) equals half the difference of the intercepted arcs. Here the far arc is 360° − (near arc), so half their difference reduces to 180° − ∠AOB.
