SAS Similarity

If two sides of one triangle are proportional to two sides of another, and the included angles between those sides are equal, then the triangles are similar (SAS ~ Side-Angle-Side).

∠D = ∠A,  DE = k·AB,  DF = k·AC  ⟹ similar

Only those three facts are forced here. The theorem guarantees the rest follows automatically: the third side comes out EF = k·BC, and the remaining angles match (∠E = ∠B, ∠F = ∠C). Every side ratio equals the same k.

Why the third side is forced

By the law of cosines, EF² = DE² + DF² − 2·DE·DF·cos∠D = k²(AB² + AC² − 2·AB·AC·cos∠A) = k²·BC². So EF is exactly k·BC, with no freedom left.

The three similarity criteria

Two triangles are similar if they share two angles (AA), two proportional sides with equal included angle (SAS), or all three sides in proportion (SSS). This explorer shows the SAS case.
