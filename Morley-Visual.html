Morley's Trisector Theorem

Trisect each of the three angles of any triangle — split each one into three equal parts. Near each side, the two trisectors closest to it (one from each end) cross at a point. Those three points P, Q, R are the surprise:

P, Q, R always form an equilateral triangle

It holds for every triangle — wildly obtuse, nearly degenerate, anything. Drag the vertices and the inner purple triangle stays equilateral.

Why it's called a "miracle"

Angle trisection is famously awkward — you can't even do it with compass and straightedge in general — so there's no obvious reason these three crossings should be so perfectly regular. That unexpected order from messy ingredients is why it's a celebrated surprise.

The Morley triangle's size

If R is the circumradius of the original triangle and its angles are A, B, C, the side of the Morley triangle is 8R·sin(A/3)·sin(B/3)·sin(C/3) — a clean formula despite the trisection.

Discovery

Frank Morley stated it around 1899. Many proofs exist today — trigonometric, algebraic, and elegant "backwards" constructions that start from an equilateral triangle and build the original around it.

A cousin you've seen

Like Napoleon's theorem, it conjures an equilateral triangle out of an arbitrary one — geometry has a soft spot for hidden equilaterals.
