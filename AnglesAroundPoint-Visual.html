Angles Around a Point

When several rays share one endpoint, they cut the space around that point into angles. Those angles always add up to a full turn:

angles around a point = 360°

It doesn't matter how many rays there are or how they're spread — going all the way around brings you back to where you started, which is one complete rotation of 360°.

Related facts

Angles on one side of a straight line (a "straight angle") add to 180° — that's half a turn. Two of those back-to-back make the full 360° around a point.

Why it matters

This is a basic angle-chasing tool: if you know all but one of the angles meeting at a point, you can find the missing one by subtracting from 360°. It also explains why the exterior angles of any polygon sum to 360° — they're the turning you do, once around.

Equal sharing

If the rays are evenly spaced, each angle is 360° ÷ (number of rays) — for example, four evenly spaced rays give four 90° right angles.
