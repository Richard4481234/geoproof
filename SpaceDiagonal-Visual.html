The Space Diagonal of a Box

A rectangular box has three edge lengths — a, b and c. The longest line you can draw inside it runs from one corner to the opposite corner, through the interior. That space diagonal has length:

d = √(a² + b² + c²)

It is the Pythagorean theorem carried into three dimensions.

The two-step proof

First find the diagonal across the base rectangle. Its legs are a and b, so by Pythagoras the face diagonal is f = √(a² + b²).

That face diagonal and the vertical edge c meet at a right angle, and together with the space diagonal they form a right triangle. Applying Pythagoras again: d = √(f² + c²) = √((a² + b²) + c²) = √(a² + b² + c²). Turn on “Right-triangle proof” to see the green face diagonal, the blue vertical edge, and the red space diagonal.

The cube case

When a = b = c, the formula collapses to d = √(3a²) = a√3 ≈ 1.732 a. Set all three sliders equal to see it.

Where it comes from

This is exactly the 3-D distance formula: the distance between opposite corners (0,0,0) and (a,b,c) is √(a² + b² + c²). It’s also the three-dimensional cousin of the British Flag theorem, and the reason a diagonal brace stiffens a rectangular frame.
